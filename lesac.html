Study Abroad Guide (From Bangladesh)

A practical roadmap: where to study (Bachelor/Masters & profession-fit), a budget range in BDT lakh (minimum → maximum), scholarship criteria, and the skills + part-time job plan you can start building now.

Golden rule

You don’t “pick a country.” You pick a plan.
  • Low risk plan: strong profile + realistic budget + clean paperwork.
  • High risk plan: weak profile + “somehow manage” budget + no clear story.
  • Fix: make a consistent story: academics → program → career outcome.
This page keeps numbers practical (not scary), but still realistic enough to avoid surprises later.

1) Where to study?

Bachelor / Masters • Profession-fit (not “trend-fit”)

Bachelor vs Masters

Choose based on timeline + profile + outcome
  • Bachelor suits students who want a long runway (study + part-time + strong career setup).
  • Masters suits students who want a faster outcome (if background matches the field).
  • Field mismatch is possible, but you need stronger proof: projects, work, or a clear explanation.
Outcome-focused choice means: “This degree + country leads to a realistic career path.”

Profession-fit checklist

Pick based on your track
  • Your profile: GPA/CGPA, gap, backlogs, work experience.
  • Your field: Business / IT / Engineering / Health / Arts.
  • City factor: bigger city = more opportunities, usually more cost.
  • Post-study plan: internships + job demand + legal work options.

Common mistake (Bangladesh)

Country-first without program logic
  • Risky: “Australia jabo / Canada jabo” but no plan.
  • Better: “I will study X because it connects to my Y background and leads to Z career.”
  • Best: You can explain your plan in 30 seconds without sounding confused.

How to stay near “minimum”

Smart choices, not risky shortcuts
  • City selection: outside mega-cities can reduce rent heavily.
  • Early planning: better scholarship and housing options.
  • Controlled lifestyle: track spending for the first 3 months.
  • Part-time readiness: skills + confidence = faster income.

What pushes you to “maximum”

Silent budget killers
  • Premium city + premium uni together.
  • Late application → fewer funding options.
  • Housing crisis zones → higher rent + larger deposits.
  • No skill plan → slower part-time start.

3) Scholarship criteria — minimum requirements + what to do now

Clear checklist + action plan

Scholarship usually looks at

Not only GPA — also your consistency and purpose
Minimum requirements (typical) Baseline
  • Academic strength: GPA/CGPA matters (higher = easier funding).
  • English score: IELTS/PTE helps both admission and funding confidence.
  • Consistency: fewer unexplained gaps/backlogs is better (but can be explained).
  • Professional documents: SOP, CV, LOR and transcripts must be clean and aligned.
What students can do now (from Bangladesh) Action
  • Build one strong proof: project, case study, research, or work sample.
  • Leadership/volunteer: responsibility-based activities show seriousness.
  • Upgrade SOP: clear story: past → program → career outcome.
  • Improve English: higher score = better overall confidence.
  • Apply early: early rounds often have more scholarship availability.
Fast checklist before applying Ready
  • Program match: your previous study and proposed subject connect clearly.
  • Career plan: one realistic target job/track after graduation.
  • Document quality: formatting, language, and consistency are strong.
  • Budget plan: your minimum plan is safe even if part-time takes time.

4) Skills + Part-time (odd jobs) — what to learn and how to start

Potential jobs • skills needed • preparation from Bangladesh

Potential student jobs (first year)

Common starter roles
  • Retail assistant (store, supermarket)
  • Restaurant/café staff (front/back)
  • Warehouse helper (packing, sorting)
  • Cleaner/support staff (commercial/residential)
  • Delivery support (depends on legal rules & city)
The first goal is not “dream job.” The first goal is stable part-time income + routine + confidence.

Skills you need (simple but powerful)

These make you employable fast
  • Spoken English basics: greeting, confirming, clarifying, apologizing, helping.
  • Customer handling: calm tone, quick solutions, polite confidence.
  • Time discipline: punctuality and shift reliability.
  • Team communication: short, clear messages with coworkers.
  • Basic digital skills: email, forms, simple apps, POS familiarity.
Communication Confidence Punctuality Teamwork Basic tech

What to start learning in Bangladesh

So you don’t feel lost after landing
  • English routine: 15 minutes daily speaking practice (topic-based).
  • CV readiness: one clean CV + one simple cover letter template.
  • Interview basics: introduce yourself, availability, strengths, and shift preference.
  • Work etiquette: professionalism, hygiene, respectful behavior, responsibility.
  • Confidence drills: practice small talk + asking for help + giving short answers.

Common mistakes (part-time)

Avoid these for a smooth start
  • Waiting for “perfect job” and losing time.
  • Not improving English then struggling with customers.
  • Irregular routine (sleep schedule, class, work balance).
  • No documentation readiness (CV, basic certificates).
  • Overconfidence without skill → workplace conflict.

Simple 30-day preparation plan

Small steps, big confidence
  • Day 1–10: speaking basics + daily self-intro + job vocabulary.
  • Day 11–20: CV + mock interviews + customer handling lines.
  • Day 21–30: routine building + confidence drills + roleplay practice.
  • Daily: 15 minutes speaking + 10 minutes reading/listening.
  • Weekly: 2 interview roleplays + 1 CV review.
  • Outcome: you become employable faster after landing.
