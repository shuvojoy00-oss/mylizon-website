The development of the London underground railway

In the first half of the 1800s, Q1London’s population grew at an astonishing rate, and the central area became increasingly congested. In addition, the expansion of the overground railway network resulted in more and more passengers arriving in the capital.

However, in 1846, a Royal Commission decided that the railways should not be allowed to enter the City, the capital’s historic and business centre. The result was that the overground railway stations formed a ring around the City. The area within consisted of poorly built, overcrowded slums and the streets were full of horse-drawn traffic. Crossing the City became a nightmare. It could take an hour and a half to travel 8 km by horse-drawn carriage or bus.

Amongst the most vocal advocates for a solution to London’s traffic problems was Charles Pearson. He saw both social and economic advantages in building an underground railway that would link the overground railway stations together and clear London slums at the same time. Q2His idea was to relocate the poor workers who lived in the inner-city slums to newly constructed suburbs, and to provide cheap rail travel for them to get to work. Pearson’s ideas gained support amongst Q3some businessmen and in 1851 he submitted a plan to Parliament.

The Metropolitan Railway Company was established in 1854. Q4The organisation had difficulty in raising the funding for such a radical and expensive scheme, Q5not least because of the critical articles printed by the press. Objectors argued that the tunnels would collapse under the weight of traffic overhead.

The construction used a technique known as ‘cut and cover’. A trench was dug, brick walls constructed, and finally a brick arch was added. Q6A two-metre-deep layer of soil was laid on top of the tunnel and the road above rebuilt.

The Metropolitan line, which opened on 10 January 1863, Q7was the world’s first underground railway. On its first day, Q8almost 40,000 passengers were carried between Paddington and Farringdon. Smoke and fumes remained a problem, Q9even though ventilation shafts were added to the tunnels.

By the 1880s, congestion on London’s streets had become worse. Q10The ‘cut and cover’ method of construction was not an option in the capital's centre. The only alternative was to tunnel deep underground.

The City & South London opened in 1890. Q11The carriages were narrow and had tiny windows just below the roof because it was thought that passengers would not want to look at walls. Q12Although it was a technical achievement, it did not make a profit. Regarding the ‘Tuppenny Tube’, Q13it was very popular, but its punctuality was not recorded.
