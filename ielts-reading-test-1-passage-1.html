The development of the London underground railway

In the first half of the 1800s, London’s population grew at an astonishing rate, and the central area became increasingly congested. In addition, the expansion of the overground railway network resulted in more and more passengers arriving in the capital.

However, in 1846, a Royal Commission decided that the railways should not be allowed to enter the City, the capital’s historic and business centre. The result was that the overground railway stations formed a ring around the City. The area within consisted of poorly built, overcrowded slums and the streets were full of horse-drawn traffic. Crossing the City became a nightmare.

Amongst the most vocal advocates for a solution to London’s traffic problems was Charles Pearson. His idea was to relocate the poor workers who lived in the inner-city slums to newly constructed suburbs, and to provide cheap rail travel for them to get to work. Pearson’s ideas gained support amongst some businessmen and in 1851 he submitted a plan to Parliament.

The Metropolitan Railway Company was established in 1854. The organisation had difficulty in raising the funding for such a radical scheme, not least because of the critical articles printed by the press.

The construction used a technique known as ‘cut and cover’. A trench was dug, brick walls constructed, and a brick arch added. A two-metre-deep layer of soil was laid on top of the tunnel and the road above rebuilt.

The Metropolitan line opened on 10 January 1863. It was the world’s first underground railway. On its first day, almost 40,000 passengers were carried, though the passage doesn't mention predictions. smoke and fumes remained a problem, even though ventilation shafts were added.

By the 1880s, congestion grew. The ‘cut and cover’ method was not an option in the capital's centre. The only alternative was to tunnel deep underground.

The City & South London opened in 1890. The carriages had tiny windows just below the roof because it was thought passengers wouldn't want to look at walls. Although it was a technical achievement, it did not make a profit. Regarding the 'Tuppenny Tube', it was very popular, but its punctuality is not mentioned.
