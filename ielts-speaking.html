Why IELTS Speaking feels scary (even if you know English)

Speaking e problem often English na. Problem holo: pressure + thinking + delivery. Examiner listens for: clarity, control, and natural communication — not “perfect grammar only”.

  • Fluency breaks: long pause, “umm”, stuck
  • Ideas disappear: “mathay kichu ashe na”
  • Examiner logic miss: answer doesn’t match question type
  • Pronunciation unclear: sounds, stress, linking

Examiner logic: what they actually test

Examiner “topic knowledge” test kore na. They test: how you communicate. That’s why memorized answers sound fake — and score drops.

  • Answer directly first (clear position)
  • Then add 2 lines: reason + small example
  • Keep it natural: short sentences, clean transitions

Speaking e “smartness” na—“control” wins.

Fluency: speed na, smoothness

Fluency mane fast kotha bola na. Fluency mane: pause control + no panic + consistent flow.

  • Use micro-pauses (not long silence)
  • Use fillers safely: “Well, honestly…”, “I’d say…” (not “umm umm”)
  • Don’t overthink grammar mid-sentence

Ideas: your brain goes blank because you try to be perfect

Most students freeze because they want the “best answer”. IELTS doesn’t need best answer. It needs a real answer that you can develop.

  • Use: 1 opinion + 2 reasons + 1 small example
  • Use BD-friendly examples (study, job, family, city life, tech)
  • Stay on the question — don’t go wide

Pronunciation: “clear” beats “British accent”

You don’t need UK accent. You need clear sounds + word stress + sentence rhythm. Most students lose marks because words don’t land clearly.

  • Fix common BD issues: /v/ vs /b/, /s/ vs /sh/, ending sounds
  • Word stress + chunking (speak in small groups)
  • Linking: not robotic, not overacting

Part 2 (Cue Card): the 60–90 second trap

Cue card e students either finish too fast, or go off-topic. Solution: simple structure + story flow.

Safe Part 2 structure

  • 1 line intro (what it is)
  • 3 points: when / where / who
  • 1 mini story / example
  • Closing line (why it mattered)

What ruins Part 2

  • Memorized “template” that sounds fake
  • No story = no flow
  • Too many complex sentences

Computer-based IELTS readiness: speaking e-o exam-day pressure real

Speaking test usually in-person, but your prep environment should feel like exam: timer, recording, and real feedback loop. Bangladesh e most students “practice” kore, but simulate kore na.

Exam-style practice (what we train)

  • Timed Part 2 + recorded playback
  • Part 3: opinion + reasoning drills
  • Feedback on clarity + pronunciation
  • Pressure-control routines
